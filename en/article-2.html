Received a Trademark Refusal? 3 Complete Remedies Explained

Principal Li Tsung-Ying March 20, 2026 ~6 min read

Receiving a trademark refusal notice can be alarming, but it does not mean the end of your application. Taiwan's trademark system provides three legal remedies to challenge a refusal ??and timing is everything.

Taiwan's Intellectual Property Office (TIPO) handles a large volume of trademark applications each year, and refusal rates are not insignificant. Descriptive marks, likelihood of confusion with earlier-filed marks, and insufficient distinctiveness are among the most common grounds for refusal. Yet many applicants, upon receiving a refusal notice, immediately abandon their application or search for an alternative mark ??unaware that with the right strategy, many refusals can be overturned.

This article details three remedy pathways ??Response, Re-examination, and Administrative Appeal ??covering when each applies, the applicable deadlines, and the key strategic factors that determine success at each stage.

Understanding Refusal Notice Types

Before entering a remedy procedure, it helps to understand the types of refusal notices TIPO issues:

  • Office Action (Pre-Refusal Notice): During initial examination, the examiner notifies the applicant of objections before issuing a formal refusal, giving the applicant one opportunity to respond.
  • Formal Refusal Decision: The examiner officially refuses the application. The clock for remedy procedures starts here.
  • Re-examination Rejection: After the applicant files for re-examination, TIPO maintains the refusal. The applicant may then proceed to administrative appeal.

Critical deadline reminder: Every remedy procedure has strict statutory deadlines ??missing them is irreversible. Response to an Office Action: 2 months from receipt (extendable). Re-examination after formal refusal: 30 days. Administrative appeal after re-examination rejection: 30 days. Act promptly.

Remedy 1: Response to Office Action

When an examiner issues a pre-refusal Office Action rather than an immediate formal refusal, this is the earliest and most cost-effective remedy opportunity ??and often the stage with the highest success rate.

1

Analyze the refusal grounds

Read the Office Action carefully. Is it a procedural issue (e.g., unclear specimen) or a substantive issue (e.g., insufficient distinctiveness, likelihood of confusion)? Different grounds call for different response strategies.

2

Prepare a response and supporting evidence

Build arguments directly against the stated grounds. If the issue is insufficient distinctiveness, submit evidence of use (advertising materials, media coverage, sales data) to establish secondary meaning. If the issue is likelihood of confusion, argue differences in appearance, concept, and pronunciation against the cited mark.

3

Consider limiting the goods/services scope if appropriate

If the refusal is based on conflict with a cited mark in certain goods or services, consider narrowing the designation to eliminate the conflict ??preserving core protection while resolving the dispute.

Remedy 2: Re-examination (After Formal Refusal)

If the examiner issues a formal refusal ??either after an unsuccessful response or directly without a pre-refusal notice ??the applicant may file for re-examination within 30 days. Re-examination is a reconsideration by the same examination authority (TIPO).

The key to a successful re-examination is reinforcement ??presenting new arguments, new evidence, or new analytical angles not covered in the original response. Simply repeating the same arguments yields very low success rates. Common reinforcement strategies include:

  • Supplementing additional evidence of use to strengthen the secondary meaning argument
  • Providing a more detailed comparison analysis demonstrating differences from the cited mark
  • Citing relevant administrative court decisions or TIPO's trademark examination guidelines to show that the present case should be treated differently
  • Presenting market context data demonstrating that actual consumer confusion is highly unlikely
"Re-examination is not an appeal ??it is an opportunity to give the examiner a new basis for reconsideration. The most effective re-examination submissions are those that illuminate facts the examiner may have overlooked."

Remedy 3: Administrative Appeal (to the Ministry of Economic Affairs)

If re-examination also fails, the applicant may file an administrative appeal with the Ministry of Economic Affairs within 30 days. An independent Appeal Review Committee conducts a fresh review ??the original examiner does not make the decision.

Administrative appeals focus more heavily on the correct application of law, so arguments must be grounded in solid legal authority:

  • Cite relevant provisions of the Trademark Act and argue whether the examiner's determination correctly applies the statutory standard
  • Reference prior administrative court decisions or Appeal Review Committee precedents as supporting authority
  • If distinctiveness is at issue, invoke TIPO's "Trademark Distinctiveness Examination Criteria" to demonstrate your mark meets the relevant standard

If the administrative appeal also fails, the applicant may further file administrative litigation with the Intellectual Property and Commercial Court (IPCC) within 2 months. While litigation is more time-consuming, for marks with significant commercial value it is often worth pursuing.

Conclusion: Act Early, Build Your Record

Success in trademark dispute resolution depends on three factors: accurate analysis of the grounds, a strong argumentative strategy, and thorough evidentiary preparation. Every case is different ??there is no universal formula ??but one principle holds across all cases: acting within the deadline is always the first step.
